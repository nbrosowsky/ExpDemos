Reaction Time Game

Task Overview

Time: This task takes approximately 5 minutes.

Compensation: You will be compensated $0.50 for completing the hit.

Task:There are two parts. In the first, you will be presented with two words in a row. The first will be presented in green font, the second in red font. Your task is to say the second word (out loud), then when it disappears, press the spacebar as quickly as possible (30 trials). In the second, you will make judgments about single words (30 trials)

Preview: You can preview this hit by continuing without accepting the hit.

To complete the hit you must accept the hit before continuing. Close the practice window, then accept the hit on the main window to continue.

Instructions:

Part 1: On every trial you'll see two words presented briefly, one after the other. Your task is to say the second word out loud. The second word will only be presented for 1 second. After the word disappears, you must press the spacebar as quickly as possible. Responses must be less than 1 second, otherwise you will receive a warning about responding too slow.

For example:

Once you submit your response (by pressing spacebar) you will automatically proceed to the next trial.

Part 2: Once you've completed the first part (30 trials), you will receive instructions about the second part, where you will make judgments about single words (30 trials). You will receive more instructions after you've completed the first part.

This experiment requires a pop-window to be full-screen. At the end of the experiment the window will automatically close and return you to amazon where you can submit the hit.

Other:

This document serves as short description of the purposes behind our experiment.

The general purpose of this study is to investigate how memory is influenced by word-meaning and the semantic relationships between words.

In the first part of the experiment you completed a simple speaking task. Each target word was preceeded by a context-word that was either the same, similar, or different than the target word. Later, your memory was tested on all the target words. Some work has shown that resolving conflicting information might help memory. Here, we were interested in examining how the relationships between the context and target words helped or hindered your later memory for the target words.
